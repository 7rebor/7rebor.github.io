A bit of addendum to the talk I gave, the section on imaging samples in the refractive index solution and it's cost. This post is EZ clear focused in that the sample is sitting in the nRIM (which I often use with CUBIC L delipidation too), but I've been imaging these samples in refractive index matched oil and have had amazing images. Standard 1.5 RI immersion oil from Sigma (https://www.sigmaaldrich.com/GB/en/product/sigma/56822) for our mesoSPIM, and RI 1.46 fused silica (https://mccroneuk.co.uk/product/fused-silica-matching-liquids-codes-50350/) for a fancy long WD objective we have for our confocal. The oils seal in the sample so the nRIM doesn't dry out, the liquids don't mix, and if any nRIM is shed from the sample, it's heavier than the oil so sinks and can be easily sucked out. The oil is also re-usable so it's cost effective. The nRIM can be diluted to the needed RI by adding extra PB. Hopefully this is helpful for someone, it's been a bit of a game changer for things over my way.

bioRxiv
WITHDRAWN: Tartrazine cannot make live tissues transparent
The authors have withdrawn their manuscript because one of the conclusions was wrong. The authors sincerely apologize to Ou et al. and the research community for the confusion. In this preprint, the authors performed an abdominal skin clearing experiment following the methods section in Ou et al. (ref. 1) and observed only a marginal clearing effect. In this condition (Figure 1), the authors detected tartrazine only in the stratum corneum (SC) of the skin, where only dead cells are present (Figure 1E, F). However, following a more detailed protocol with additional tips kindly provided by Ou et al. (ref. 1), the abdominal skin became more transparent. It seems that mechanical damage to the SC barrier is critical for successful clearing. In this condition, the authors detected strong tartrazine signals under the SC, including the dermis where live cells are located. We are still concerned about the issue of osmolality and toxicity to live cells; cleared cells must be under hypertonic conditions (Figure 3 and 4). However, since Figure 1 (especially Figure 1E, F) no longer stands, and the title can be misinterpreted as if the original results (ref. 1) were irreproducible, the authors believe it is best to withdraw this preprint. Therefore, the authors do not wish this work to be cited as reference for the project. *Thread Reply:* My experience (from doing a lot of iDISCO+ on mouse brains and some vDISCO / wildDISCO on whole mice) is that the 488nm channel is blurrier and much more autofluorescent than the ones further red, so some depth-dependent blurring is to be expected.
