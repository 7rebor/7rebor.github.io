*Thread Reply:* We tend to go with the cylindrical lens-based approach because photobleaching scales non-linearly with the laser intensity, and the peak power of a digitally scanned light-sheet can be quite high (especially with pulsed laser sources).

Both require galvos, but linear galvos are much easier to come by. Especially for ASLM, you need fast galvos, so resonant galvos are preferred. Likewise, you will begin to run into challenges if you are doing large field of view imaging with a digitally scanned light-sheet...

Aligning a digitally scanned light-sheet is practically easier in my opinion, since the beam can be evaluated with a shear plate interferometer much more readily.

As for shadow artifacts, I'm not sure if there has ever been a real quantitative comparison between the two methods. I think you can largely consider them similar in performance. The higher the NA of the illumination beam, the better both methods will work though.

Being able to tune the size of your field of view, as @Rob Lees mentioned, is a nice advantage of digitally scanned.

*Thread Reply:* Some great points have already been made. Here a few others.

Advantages of DSLM: More flexible in terms of light sheet height. More even illumination across the height Possibility of slit detection, but this does not really work across large samples as the slit size is very critical (either no effect or loss of signal). Advantages of SPIM: Cheaper (cylindrical lens vs. galvo) and easier to build/align Lower peak power and, therefore, gentler on the samples and much less likely to saturate the fluorophores It can be arbitrarily fast. *Thread Reply:* Great question. It depends very much on the way the stack is taken. On some commercial systems, a stack is taken by moving the stage in steps and stopping for each plane. In this case your section is basically the light sheet thickness. You want to have sufficient overlap, and half the thickness for delta z is a good idea. Otherwise, you may be missing information, and the side view of the data would be stretched or stripy. In our case (and on many other setups), we sweep the light sheet through the sample and take a movie of this process in order to be maximally fast. Hence, the stage does not stop for each plane but moves at a constant speed. This speed is determined by your frame rate and the desired z-spacing. However, in this case, your section is your light sheet thickness + the distance the stage has moved during the camera exposure, equal to your z spacing. Hence, the larger you make your z spacing, the more you smear out your sheet and the thicker your section. In this case, you will always caption everything, no black planes; but larger spacing means worse sectioning.
