OPM ("single objective light sheet microscopy") has developed into a mature technology that compliments very well the existing multi-lens light sheet setups. Still, why do we often start our papers (and videos) by listing the alleged disadvantages of existing techniques? Because we could otherwise not publish our new invention? Multi-objective lens light sheet has been proven to be very effective and robust for many applications. The often required new sample embedding techniques have inspired scientists to rethink their current sample embedding and people have started new research fields away fro the cover glass. OPM is a branch of light sheet microscopy that has its niche. But there are also a few drawbacks and limitations that the readers should be informed about. And the name "single" objective light sheet microscopy is misleading and not "much simpler". So why not starting a video or paper on a positive note and explaining which niche the new technique fills and how it builds on other successful techniques...

*Thread Reply:* We received the 3i CTLS, I am very impressed with it, especially considering it is an ex-demo system and has been around the block more than a few times.

Pros: • Mounting is simple (bondic glue, or clamp from two sides) and the sample holder sits very nicely in place with magnets. • Extremely easy to find the sample using a pre-scan and then a 3D pre-scan to work out the extents of the lateral tiling you should do. • Setting up multiple channels is intuitive, just tick or untick the boxes • Easy post-objective zoom module to change sampling of pixels without doing any objective changes • Only need to change objective when going to really high resolution (sub 1-micron) • Works with many imaging mediums • No dipping of objectives in any medium, less cleaning • Can easily view the sample at any time with your eyes (this is said coming from a Leica DLS, where it is impossible to see the sample - we have a black box around the scope) • Software is easy for making quick movies, downsampling options, testing merges before doing the final one • Can export as other file types if needed, also able to load the native .sldy files in bioformats for e.g. ImageJ Cons: • Software is a little buggy when it comes to prescan and positioning of the final acquisition, i.e. define it in one place, but it starts imaging in another - they are aware and are very open to feedback and making changes. NOTE: this doesn't always happen • Open-top reservoir is a little prone to spillage • Glass coverslip sits on top of reservoir and if you get medium on it, it needs cleaning, which takes time and can be frustrating • Need to re-calibrate the SLM every time you want to acquire images at a different zoom or refractive index. I.e. there is no way to save these files for each combo and just load up a previous file from a previous SLM calibration - they will work on this, I'm sure • No user config files saved for the software, so can't save settings for an acquisition to return to the next day There are probably more, happy to show it/talk about it to anyone who wants to see it, by Zoom or in person.

*Thread Reply:* Have you all heard of Bondic? It is great for BABB and CUBIC cleared samples (that I've tested so far, probably everything). It is a UV-curing glue, you put it down then shine UV light at it for 4 seconds (preferably more) and it sets. Most immersion media will soften the glue so that when you are finished the sample comes off the glue and the glue can be peeled off the holder. It is like UV-cured plastic, so good!!

https://www.amazon.co.uk/stores/BONDICUK/page/A8E68F6C-591F-4D36-8B6B-3FDD0FEB396C?ref_=ast_bln

Cyanoacrylate glue can be a bit temperamental because it is sticky straight away, takes a little while to dry, and seems to be very fluorescent (Bondic probably is too, but I've never noticed it affecting the sample much), it also seems to seep in to the sample a bit, which is irreversible. But I've only tried cyanoacrylate once or twice from my limited experience, and moved on to Bondic.

A benefit of Bondic is that you can position samples at odd angles and then set the glue, to fix them in place. That is usually beneficial for getting the right detection axis through the thinnest part (or wherever is best).

*Thread Reply:* Took a little bit longer than I hoped, but here is what we got after trying FastGreen FCF in live samples. This image shows a single slice through a collagen hydrogel (made of human collagen I; VitroCol from Advanced Biomatrix) with human T cells (A3R5.7 cells stably expressing LifeAct-EGFP) embedded in it. This is using an iSPIM light sheet microscope, with single-sided (hence the shadow streaks!) illumination with a 640 nm laser.

Exposure settings had to be quite harsh because the signal was very very dim. However, you can see that the collagen fibers do indeed light up, which was nice to see! Unfortunately, what we also see a lot of is that some of the cells seem to pick up the dye as well, and this dye that is taken up by cells actually appears quite bright. I picked a relatively cell-sparse slice to show the fibers. You are seeing the top couple of hundred microns of the hydrogel, with the top being on the right. The blurriness you see on the right is not real, my light sheet was not very well aligned at the time...

The FastGreen FCF staining was done as follows: • Dissolved the powder in sterile PBS at 100 mg/mL • Added the dye at 100 ug/mL directly into the gel/cell mixture while the gel was forming • Allowed the gel to form as normal, immersed hydrogel in (phenol red-free) medium, and imaged the next day We also tried the dye at 1 mg/mL but saw no improvement in signal intensity. At this point I just don't think it works very well in unfixed samples and I am probably not going to continue using it, unless I have a student looking for a quick project and want them to try out a few more conditions... but not sure what else I would try. Maybe going even lower in concentration, in case the dye is self-quenching? Maybe dissolving it in DMSO? Ideas welcome!

*Thread Reply:* We tend to go with the cylindrical lens-based approach because photobleaching scales non-linearly with the laser intensity, and the peak power of a digitally scanned light-sheet can be quite high (especially with pulsed laser sources).

Both require galvos, but linear galvos are much easier to come by. Especially for ASLM, you need fast galvos, so resonant galvos are preferred. Likewise, you will begin to run into challenges if you are doing large field of view imaging with a digitally scanned light-sheet...

Aligning a digitally scanned light-sheet is practically easier in my opinion, since the beam can be evaluated with a shear plate interferometer much more readily.

As for shadow artifacts, I'm not sure if there has ever been a real quantitative comparison between the two methods. I think you can largely consider them similar in performance. The higher the NA of the illumination beam, the better both methods will work though.

Being able to tune the size of your field of view, as @Rob Lees mentioned, is a nice advantage of digitally scanned.

*Thread Reply:* Some great points have already been made. Here a few others.

Advantages of DSLM: • More flexible in terms of light sheet height. • More even illumination across the height • Possibility of slit detection, but this does not really work across large samples as the slit size is very critical (either no effect or loss of signal). Definitely a loss of signal along beam propagation as the beam is scattered, leading to uneven illumination. • Some people claim that striping is reduced, but I am not sure about this. Advantages of SPIM: • Cheaper (cylindrical lens vs. galvo) and easier to build/align • Lower peak power and, therefore, gentler on the samples and much less likely to saturate the fluorophores • It can be arbitrarily fast. No need to scan, and one can strobe the sheet if you want to freeze motion. The framerate depends only on the camera. • Easier to implement mSPIM (pivoting of the light sheet to reduce striping)
