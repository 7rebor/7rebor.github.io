Things like sonication, heat, and shaking are being used to in CUBIC to improve speed I think. Given this, I'm curious if you or others have tested switching from CUBIC to other aqueous methods, like Optimus or iTOMEI-B for light sheet. In comparison to CUBIC, Optimus and iTOMEI-B seem to offer advantages of speed and tissue quality in publication - with similar transparency - and do so in my hands for various marine invertebrates - but so far I've tested it only in a confocal and not yet light sheet context.

Cephalopods have a lot of skin pigmentation - and CUBIC does great with the non-melanin pigments in cephalopods - and it has been my go to method for several years now. However, while it was relatively fast for passive aqueous methods at its inception, CUBIC is now fairly slow compared to more recent methods. Two that I like as CUBIC alternatives are Optimus and iTOMEI-B - as the clearing process is faster - and Optimus you can clear and mount in the same solution, which simplifies things even more. Optimus also offers the advantage not changing the tissue volume. significantly. I haven't considered it from the standpoint of financial cost across methods - but the gains in time, tissue volume, and protocol simplification seem really useful - and the transparency seems competitive to other methods. Like CUBIC, Optimus is also commercially available - from Crayon - which is nice but not critical. So I've largely switched from CUBIC to Optimus or iTOMEI-B this year - but I haven't tried it in a light sheet system yet - just slides on a confocal so far.

Overall, are there advantages of CUBIC / disadvantages of others like Optimus or iTOMEI-B for light sheet that people have run into - and so the methods aren't being adopted for light sheet - or are they are still fairly new and being explored?

A bit of addendum to the talk I gave, the section on imaging samples in the refractive index solution and it's cost. This post is EZ clear focused in that the sample is sitting in the nRIM (which I often use with CUBIC L delipidation too), but I've been imaging these samples in refractive index matched oil and have had amazing images. Standard 1.5 RI immersion oil from Sigma (https://www.sigmaaldrich.com/GB/en/product/sigma/56822) for our mesoSPIM, and RI 1.46 fused silica (https://mccroneuk.co.uk/product/fused-silica-matching-liquids-codes-50350/) for a fancy long WD objective we have for our confocal. The oils seal in the sample so the nRIM doesn't dry out, the liquids don't mix, and if any nRIM is shed from the sample, it's heavier than the oil so sinks and can be easily sucked out. The oil is also re-usable so it's cost effective. The nRIM can be diluted to the needed RI by adding extra PB. Hopefully this is helpful for someone, it's been a bit of a game changer for things over my way.

*Thread Reply:* We tested a few approaches for spheroids clearing:

1) EZClear method with spheroids embedded in agarose and directly incubated in EZView solution (without the THF pretreatment). However the EZView solution is very expensive. Immunofluorescence is done before the clearing.

2) Clearing protocol from Hans Clevers lab also works well, but is fructose/glycerol based. The clearing solution is viscous and sticky, but cost effective.

https://www.nature.com/articles/s41596-019-0160-8#Sec10

3) 2ECi clearing with 1-propanol gradient dehydration and ECi RI matching solution. https://journals.biologists.com/dev/article/146/3/dev166884/48939/Broad-applicability-of-a-streamlined-ethyl Cost effective, non toxic, work well.
